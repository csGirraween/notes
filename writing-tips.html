Writing Tips for Selective High School Placement Test

Marking Criteria for Selective High School Placement Test Writing Component

In Selective, each candidate's writing response is independently evaluated by two trained examiners. Each examiner assigns a score out of 25, and the combined total provides a score out of 50.

The examiners assess responses based on several key aspects:

  • Content and Relevance: The degree to which the response addresses the prompt effectively and remains on topic.
  • Structure and Organisation: The clarity and logical flow of ideas, including paragraphing and overall coherence.
  • Language Use: The range and accuracy of vocabulary and sentence structures employed.
  • Mechanics: Correctness in spelling, punctuation, and grammar.

For illustrative examples of marked responses with examiner comments, refer to the provided sample answers document.

Writing Styles Assessed

Below are various writing styles that may be evaluated:

Definition: Aims to convince the reader of a particular viewpoint or to take a specific action.

Structure:

  • Introduction: Presents the topic and states the writer's position clearly. Keep it interesting yet informative.
  • Body Paragraphs: Each focuses on a single argument supporting the position, backed by evidence or examples. We highly suggest the PEEL structure, refer to notes that would have been taken in classes!
  • Conclusion: Summarises the main points and reinforces the writer's stance. Add a call to action if possible.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Use strong, emotive language to persuade. Use persuasive and figurative language whenever applicable.
  • Add evidence/anecodotes/examples/quotes and keep it relevant and realistic.
  • Refer to any notes and examples we have written during class, these are high quality responses that should be definitely used for inspiration.

Definition: Tells a story, real or imagined.

Structure:

  • Orientation: Introduces characters, setting and context.
  • Complication: Presents a problem or conflict.
  • Resolution: Details how the problem is resolved.
  • Conclusion: Brings a nice wrap up to the text and leaves readers satisfied.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Develop engaging characters and a vivid setting.
  • Build tension leading up to the climax.
  • Show, don't just tell! Use descriptive/figurative language and sensory details.

Definition: Retells events or experiences in chronological order.

Structure:

  • Introduction: Sets the scene by providing context (who, what, when, where).
  • Series of Events: Describes the events in the order they occurred.
  • Conclusion: Reflects on the events or provides a personal comment.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Maintain clarity by following a logical sequence.
  • Use past tense consistently.
  • Include personal reflections to add depth.

Definition: Provides information on a particular topic, event, or issue, typically in a journalistic style.

Note: This can come as both a narrative based or persuasive based writing!

Structure:

  • Headline: Captures attention and summarises the main idea.
  • Lead Paragraph: Summarises or introduces the key points of the article.
  • Body Paragraphs: Elaborate on the details, including quotes, statistics and background information.
  • Conclusion: Wraps up the article, sometimes with a call to action or a look ahead.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Use an objective and formal tone.
  • Prioritise information using the inverted pyramid structure, most important details first.
  • Ensure the text remains engaging.

Definition: Communicates information, thoughts, or feelings to a specific person or group.

Note: This can come as both a narrative based or persuasive based writing!

Structure:

  • Salutation: Greeting the recipient (e.g., "Dear [Name],").
  • Body: The main content of the letter. This can be further structured into a complication and resolution where relevant.
  • Closing: A polite sign-off (e.g., "Sincerely," "Best regards,").

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Adjust the tone and formality based on the recipient and purpose.
  • Be clear and concise.
  • Proofread for grammatical accuracy and clarity.

Definition: Explains or informs about a topic in a straightforward, logical manner.

Structure:

  • Introduction: Introduces the topic and presents the thesis statement.
  • Body Paragraphs: Each focuses on a specific aspect of the topic, providing facts and explanations.
  • Conclusion: Summarises the information and reinforces the thesis.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Use clear and precise language.
  • Organise information logically.
  • Avoid personal opinions; focus on factual information.

Definition: Offers guidance or recommendations on a particular subject to help the reader make informed decisions.

Structure:

  • Title: Clearly indicates the topic of advice.
  • Introduction: Briefly outlines the purpose of the advice.
  • Body: Provides detailed recommendations, often in bullet points or numbered steps.
  • Conclusion: Encourages the reader to follow the advice and may include additional resources.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Be direct and to the point.
  • Use imperative verbs to convey instructions.
  • Ensure the advice is practical and actionable.

Definition: A personal record of daily events, thoughts and feelings.

Structure:

  • Salutation (optional): E.g., "Dear Diary,"
  • Body: Describes events and personal reflections.
  • Closing: A concluding thought or remark.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Write in the first person to reflect personal experiences.
  • Express emotions and thoughts candidly. Use emotive language!
  • Maintain a consistent voice and style.

Definition: Provides instructions or information to help the reader understand or accomplish something.

Structure:

  • Title: Clearly states what the guide is about.
  • Introduction: Explains the purpose and scope of the guide.
  • Body: Breaks down the process or information into clear, manageable steps or sections.
  • Conclusion: Summarises the key points and may offer additional tips.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Use headings and subheadings for easy navigation.
  • Include visuals if they aid understanding.
  • Ensure instructions are sequential and clear.

Definition: An exploration of different perspectives on a particular issue, leading to a reasoned conclusion.

Structure:

  • Introduction: Presents the issue and its significance.
  • Body Paragraphs: Each examines a different viewpoint or argument related to the issue.
  • Conclusion: Weighs the arguments presented and states a reasoned conclusion.

Tips for Effective Writing:

  • Present arguments impartially, supporting each with evidence.
  • Use linking words to contrast different viewpoints (e.g., however, on the other hand).
  • Maintain a balanced analysis, acknowledging the strengths and weaknesses of each perspective.
  • Conclude with a well-reasoned judgment or recommendation based on the discussion.

Language Techniques

Figurative language involves using words or expressions with meanings different from their literal interpretation to create vivid imagery or emphasise ideas. There are many! But key types include:

Metaphor: A direct comparison between two unrelated things, stating that one thing is another, to suggest they share common characteristics.

Example: "Time is a thief."

Explanation: This implies that time stealthily takes away life's moments, much like a thief would steal possessions.

Simile: A comparison between two different things using "like" or "as" to highlight a shared quality.

Example: "Her smile was as bright as the sun."

Explanation: This suggests that her smile is exceptionally radiant, comparable to the brightness of the sun.

Allusion: An indirect reference to a person, event, or piece of literature, allowing readers to make connections.

Example: "He has the Midas touch when it comes to business."

Explanation: This alludes to King Midas from Greek mythology, implying that everything he handles turns to gold, or is highly successful.

Hyperbole: An intentional exaggeration for emphasis or comedic effect.

Example: "I've told you a million times."

Explanation: This emphasises frequent repetition, though not literally a million times.

Personification: Attributing human characteristics to non-human entities or inanimate objects.

Example: "The wind whispered through the trees."

Explanation: This gives the wind the human ability to whisper, suggesting a gentle rustling sound.

Persuasive language is used to convince the audience of a particular point of view or to take specific action. Key techniques include:

Modality: Refers to the strength or certainty conveyed by the language, often through modal verbs.

Example: "We must address climate change immediately."

Explanation: The use of "must" indicates a high degree of necessity and urgency.

Rhetorical Questions: Questions posed for effect, not requiring an answer, to provoke thought.

Example: "Isn't it time we took a stand for justice?"

Explanation: Encourages the audience to agree with the implied assertion that action is overdue.

Statistics and Evidence: Using data and factual information to support an argument.

Example: "Studies show that 90% of students benefit from regular exercise."

Explanation: Provides concrete data to substantiate the claim about exercise benefits.

Examples: Specific instances that illustrate a point or support an argument.

Example: "For instance, countries that have implemented renewable energy have seen economic growth."

Explanation: Offers a real-world example to back the argument for renewable energy.

Anecdotes: Short, personal stories used to illustrate a point or evoke empathy.

Example: "When I switched to a plant-based diet, my energy levels soared."

Explanation: Personal experience used to support the benefits of dietary change.

Emotive language involves word choices that aim to elicit an emotional response from the audience, often to persuade or influence opinions.

Example: "The innocent children were mercilessly slaughtered."

Explanation: Words like "innocent" and "mercilessly slaughtered" evoke strong feelings of sympathy and outrage.

Example: "Join us in this noble cause to uplift the underprivileged."

Explanation: Words like "noble" and "uplift" inspire a sense of duty and compassion.
